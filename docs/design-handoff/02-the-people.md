# 02 — The People

Designers, meet the real humans this product is for. These are fictional but built from interview and customer research patterns. Use them as your protagonists.

---

## 2.1 Marcus — the agency principal

**Marcus Tan, 41. Managing partner at Hone & Tan, a 14-person creative-strategy agency in Sydney.**

He started Hone & Tan with his university roommate eleven years ago. Four people in a Surry Hills warehouse. They're now 14, doing brand strategy and growth retainers for tech and CPG clients. Revenue around AUD $4.8M. He owns 55%.

Marcus is the kind of operator who lives in his calendar and dies by his Notion. He uses HubSpot for the sales pipeline, Notion for everything internal, Slack for team comms, Linear for the dev sub-team they incubated, Toggl for time tracking, Stripe for invoicing, Xero for accounting, and Google Workspace for everything else. The team uses ChatGPT a lot, but in scattered ways.

His current pain: **he is the bottleneck for every commercial decision in the business**. Whether to take on a new client, whether to push back on a scope change, whether to raise prices, whether to hire — all of it lands on him because he's the one who carries the whole context in his head. He has not had a clean weekend in six months.

He found VitaOS through a YC Hacker News post. He installed it on a Saturday night, drinking a Negroni in his kitchen. He set up Tom and Tim, connected HubSpot and Slack, and asked: "What did Sarah Kim from Acme actually want last week?"

Tom answered correctly in 4 seconds, citing the email thread, the Slack thread, and the call transcript. Marcus messaged his partner: "I think I just had a religious experience."

**What Marcus needs from the design:**

- The Operator App must feel like *his* command center. He's running a business, not a side project. Premium, not playful.
- The first 10 minutes must feel like setting up an Eames lounge chair, not configuring a Cisco router.
- He'll be on his laptop. He's a Mac person. He has retina-class taste — he notices when a font tracking is slightly off.
- He uses the keyboard. Cmd-K must do *the right thing*. Don't bury power features.
- He'll demo the product to other agency principals. The screens must screenshot like a magazine.

**What Marcus needs from Tom (the personal app):**

- On his phone, glanceable. He's between meetings, on the tram, putting his kid to bed.
- Voice should work in the car. He's commuting an hour each way.
- Approvals must take two taps. Anything more and he'll just say "fuck it, send it" and lose the audit trail.
- Tom should sound like a competent associate, not a butler. Marcus hates obsequious chatbots.

**What Marcus needs from Tim:**

- A weekly Monday-morning team brief he can read in 3 minutes.
- A view of which of his three senior people are over-extended.
- Confidence that Tim is not surveilling individual team members — he wants his team to feel safe.

**What kills Marcus's trust:**

- Tom getting basic facts wrong about clients.
- A scattered, "where do I find that again" navigation.
- A "loading..." spinner with no indication of what's happening.
- An approval card that doesn't show *why* Tom wants to do this.
- Anything that feels like enterprise software from 2014.

## 2.2 Priya — the senior associate

**Priya Anand, 29. Senior account director at Hone & Tan.**

She joined two years ago from a bigger agency. She owns four client relationships and has a junior reporting to her. She lives in her phone, her laptop, and a Moleskine. Her workday is six client meetings, three internal stand-ups, two strategy sprints, and a stream of "quick questions" from her junior and from clients.

She works 8:30 to 6:30, refuses to look at Slack after 7 PM (mostly), and is currently studying for her MBA part-time.

Priya is Tom's *daily-driver* user. The Operator App is Marcus's tool. Tom is hers.

Priya's day with Tom:

- **7:50 AM** — On the train. Reads Tom's morning brief on her phone. Three minutes. Knows what's happened across her four clients overnight.
- **9:15 AM** — Between her first two meetings. Asks Tom: "what did I commit to with Bree on Friday?" Tom answers with the exact paragraph from the call transcript.
- **11:30 AM** — Tom pings her: "Bree just emailed asking for the deck. I've drafted a reply that includes the v2 deck link from yesterday's Notion. Approve to send?" She taps approve. Done.
- **3:45 PM** — She's about to write a long status email to a client. She voice-notes Tom: "Status email for Carillon, this week's progress, next week's plan, soft escalation on the brief sign-off." Tom returns a draft in 30 seconds. She edits two lines and sends.
- **6:00 PM** — On the way home. Tom: "Tomorrow's prep — your 9 AM with Acme is on Q3 planning. Here's what changed since the last meeting." She reads it on the train. Walks into tomorrow's meeting prepared.

**What Priya needs from the design:**

- Tom must feel like *her* assistant, not the firm's surveillance system. The privacy boundary is emotional, not just technical.
- The mobile app must work one-handed on a tram. Thumb-reach matters.
- Voice input should feel like talking to a thoughtful colleague, not dictating into a robot.
- Tom must remember context within a conversation. If she says "the Acme deck" and then "send it" — Tom should know what "it" is.
- The aesthetic must feel a generation ahead of every other work app. She's a creative — she has standards.

## 2.3 Linda — the CPA partner

**Linda Okoye-Mendez, 53. Managing partner at Mendez & Co, a 6-person tax and advisory practice in Phoenix, Arizona.**

She's done this for 27 years. She came up in Big Four, made partner at a mid-size firm in her 40s, then opened her own shop. Her clients are restaurant owners, dentists, construction GCs, real estate developers. Average client is doing $2M – $40M in revenue.

She uses Drake Tax, QuickBooks, a client portal called TaxDome, Microsoft 365, and a WhatsApp business chat that is *the* primary communication channel with most of her clients. Half her clients refuse to use email.

She's not a tech enthusiast. She's not an AI skeptic — she's actually quite curious. She just doesn't have time for products that don't work.

She found VitaOS through her son-in-law, who works in tech. He set it up for her one weekend. She connected Drake, QuickBooks, TaxDome, and her email. She added the `pack-cpa` ontology pack.

Her first month with VitaOS:

- She set the autonomy to L1 (read-only) for the first three weeks. Tom watched. Tom answered questions. Tom didn't act.
- After three weeks of zero hallucinations, she switched the default to L2.
- Tom started filing notes against client engagement records as she dictated them.
- Two months in, she switched to L3 for client communications. She approves drafts. Saves her 90 minutes a day.
- She has never touched the policy editor or the block catalog. She doesn't need to.

**What Linda needs from the design:**

- The Operator App must be *learnable*. She doesn't read docs. She figures things out by clicking.
- Onboarding must build trust in steps. She is *not* going to give an AI access to her client communications on day 1.
- The autonomy switcher must be *obvious*. She wants to dial it up and down without feeling like she's flipping a circuit breaker.
- Tom on her phone must use *her vocabulary*, not Silicon Valley vocabulary. "Engagement," "workpaper," "scope," "extension."
- Compliance and audit trail must be visible at every step. She is professionally liable for what she sends to clients.

**What Linda doesn't need:**

- A workflow editor. She'll never use it.
- An MCP server. Doesn't know what it is.
- A pack SDK. She installed pack-cpa once and forgot it exists.
- Anything that looks "techy." Her firm's brand is "trusted, experienced, calm." The tool should match.

Linda is the user who tells us if the design is *truly* learnable. If she gets stuck, we've failed.

## 2.4 Daniel — the reseller agency operator

**Daniel Levrai, 34. Founder of Levrai Group, a 4-person consultancy that productizes AI deployments for professional services firms.**

He found VitaOS in our public Discord. Two days later he'd installed it on a server he runs in Cologne, rebranded it as "Tessera" for his agency, and resold it to his first client — a 22-person property management firm in Hamburg.

Daniel is the user that turns VitaOS into a 100x business. He'll deploy us to 10, 20, 50 firms over the next year if we make it easy.

**What Daniel needs:**

- White-label that is *complete*. Not just a logo swap. Agent names. Color system. Domain. Email DKIM/SPF/DMARC. Onboarding pages.
- A reseller billing model where his clients pay him, he pays us a wholesale rate.
- A client portal experience he can demo on a 9-screen sales call without us showing through.
- Per-client autonomy presets so he can ship "VitaOS for Property Managers" with sensible defaults.
- A pack-publishing flow so he can sell his proprietary industry packs.

**What Daniel will demand from the design:**

- The white-label config screens must be *gorgeous*. They are his moat.
- The brand-customization preview must show his branding live as he configures it.
- The client onboarding (the white-labeled version) must look indistinguishable from a high-end bespoke product.

## 2.5 Aisha — the new associate

**Aisha Bhandari, 24. Junior account executive, started at Hone & Tan three weeks ago.**

Marcus's firm onboarded her using Tom. He gave her a workspace seat on day one. Her first task: "Use Tom for everything. Ask questions you'd normally ask Priya or me. We'll know if you're using it."

By week 3, Aisha:
- Knows the names of every client Hone & Tan has ever worked with.
- Knows the politics of every active relationship (Bree at Acme prefers Slack, hates calls).
- Has filed her own notes against five client records.
- Has drafted three external emails that Priya approved without edits.

She didn't need a 90-day ramp. She needed a great tool.

**What Aisha needs from the design:**

- Onboarding for a *new team member* (different from onboarding the workspace).
- A "first 30 days" mode where Tom is extra-helpful with context.
- An obvious way to see who else is on her team, who owns what, who to ping for what.
- A trustworthy way to ask "dumb questions" without it being on a Slack thread.

She's the user who proves VitaOS isn't just a tool — it's organizational intelligence.

## 2.7 Anti-personas (people we are *not* designing for)

It helps to know who we are *not* serving:

- **The ChatGPT user** who just wants generic chat with no setup. We have setup; that's the value.
- **The enterprise IT buyer** at a 5,000-person bank. We are not Microsoft Copilot. We are not SailPoint. We are not a procurement-driven sale.
- **The engineer who wants to build their own** with LangChain or Crew or LangGraph. We have an SDK, but our primary user is not building agents — they're using them.
- **The hobbyist** who wants AI to summarize their grocery list. We are a business platform.

When in doubt, design for Marcus, Priya, Linda. Build white-label power for Daniel. Make Aisha feel welcome.
